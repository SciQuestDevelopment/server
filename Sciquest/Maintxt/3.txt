Abstract: Atomically dispersed catalysts, with maximized atom utilization of expensive metal components and relatively stable ligand structures, offer high reactivity and selectivity. However, the formation of atomic‚Äêscale metals without aggregation remains a formidable challenge due to thermodynamic stabilization driving forces. Here, a top‚Äêdown process is presented that starts from iron nanoparticles, using dual‚Äêmetal interbonds (RhÔ£øFe bonding) as a chemical facilitator to spontaneously convert Fe nanoparticles to single atoms at low temperatures. The presence of RhÔ£øFe bonding between adjacent Fe and Rh single atoms contributes to the thermodynamic stability, which facilitates the stripping of a single Fe atom from the Fe nanoparticles, leading to the stabilized single atom. The dual single‚Äêatom Rh‚ÄìFe catalyst renders excellent electrocatalytic performance for the hydrogen evolution reaction in an acidic electrolyte. This discovery of dual‚Äêmetal interbonding as a chemical facilitator paves a novel route for atomic dispersion of chemical metals and the design of efficient catalysts at the atomic scale.

Main: Heterogeneous catalysis is pivotal to the modern chemical industry,[1] with many heterogeneous catalysts comprising transition or noble metals deposited over a solid support phase.[2] Dispersing metals to the ultrasmall forms is an effective strategy to increase their usage efficiency and catalytic reactivity.[3, 4] Recently, atomic dispersion of metal atoms has been realized over the support to form single‚Äêatom catalysts (SACs),[4-8] which attract considerable attention due to their maximized metal usage and improved active site homogeneity.[9, 10] Because of the high surface energy, single metal atoms are generally formed with the existence of particles or clusters as the byproducts, prohibiting stable atomic dispersion.[11-14] As a result, it is of great importance to find efficient technologies for increasing atomic dispersion while maintaining high stability and catalytic activity during applications.

Typically, two strategies are used to disperse SACs stably. One is the bottom‚Äêup method, which conventionally refers to single atoms derived from metal ions. Metal ions are captured in electronic and/or structural defects[15-17] of solid matrix such as metal oxide,[18-22] metal carbides,[23, 24] zeolites/metal‚Äìorganic frameworks,[25-28] or carbon‚Äêbased materials.[29, 30] The anchored metal ions rely on the substrates to prevent the migration, collision, and coalescence of nanoparticles,[31] stabilizing the synthesized single atoms to some extent. Another method is the top‚Äêdown strategy, which directly focuses on the conversion of metal nanoparticles to single atoms, minimizing the concerns of nanoparticles as a byproduct in the bottom‚Äêup method. The means to strip a single atom from metal nanoparticles (NPs) are significant. Recently, high temperature was shown to successfully transform noble metal nanoparticles to single atoms.[31] Besides the high‚Äêtemperature mean, few approaches have sought to make use of the way, which is required for single atom generation at low temperature due to thermodynamic stability.

Herein, we propose the dual‚Äêmetal (RhÔ£øFe) interbond as a novel chemical facilitator to convert Fe nanoparticles at low temperature to the corresponding single atoms. The existence of Fe hinders the formation of Rh nanoparticles even before pyrolysis. Essentially, the formation of RhÔ£øFe bonding facilitates the bonded Fe atoms to be stripped from metal nanoparticles, and enhances the stability of the as‚Äêformed single metal atoms. First principles calculations suggest that the process of atomic dispersion is derived from the formation of an intermediate Fe cluster‚ÄêFe‚ÄìN4, which induce the formation of adjacent carbon vacancy for capturing the mobile Rh. The following formation of RhÔ£øFe bonding significantly contributes to thermodynamic stability, promoting the dispersion of single metal atoms and enhancing the stability of Fe/Rh single atoms. Owing to the stabilized Rh and Fe single‚Äêatom sites, the catalyst exhibits high HER activity with an overpotential of 36 mV at 10 mA cm‚àí2, a Tafel slope of 26 mV dec‚àí1 and excellent cycling stability, exceeding the activity of commercial Pt/C.

The metal species are embedded on the nitrogen‚Äêdoped carbon hollow spheres (NCS) (Figures S1 and S2, Supporting Information). As the precursor of the Fe/Rh‚Äêanchored NCS (FR‚ÄêNCS), the Fe‚Äêanchored NCS (F‚ÄêNCS) sample possesses the majority of Fe species as the Fe nanoparticles rather than Fe SACs without adding Rh (Figure 1a,b). The signal at g = 3.03 as the fingerprint of the atomic Fe‚ÄìN4 moieties[32] in the EPR spectrum (Figure S3, Supporting Information) is not observed in F‚ÄêNCS. The sextets and the singlet in M√ñssbauer spectroscopy corresponding to metallic iron[33] (Figure 1c), account for over 92% of Fe species in the F‚ÄêNCS. The metallic Fe (PDF #06‚Äê0696) as the predominant phase in F‚ÄêNCS is also confirmed by XRD (Figure 3h). The use of 5 vol% H2 facilitates to form metallic Fe at 200 ¬∞C rather than iron oxides,[34, 35] confirming the efficient reduction effect of 5 vol% H2/95 vol% Ar (Figure S4, Supporting Information). In addition, the dominating Rh nanoparticles are formed in the Rh‚Äêanchored NCS (R‚ÄêNCS, Figure 1d) without adding Fe, which is confirmed by TEM (Figure 1e) and XPS of Rh 3d spectrum (Figure 1f). In sharp comparison, the co‚Äêexistence of Rh and Fe facilitates the metal nanoparticles transforming to atomically dispersed Rh and Fe atoms (Figure 1g,h). The XPS confirms that the Rh exists as the oxidative state Rh3+ rather than the metallic state (upper panel in Figure 1i; Note S1, Supporting Information) when Rh < 3.5 at% (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The doublets in M√ñssbauer spectroscopy demonstrates that over 89% of Fe atoms are atomically dispersed in the form of atomic Fe‚ÄìN4 (the lower panel in Figure 1i). These indicate that an interactive effect between Rh and Fe can induce each metal to stabilize and facilitate the formation of metal single‚Äêatom sites.

This interactive effect between Rh and Fe reflects in the local structure. Followed by a step‚Äêby‚Äêstep impregnation process, Rh and Fe with mass loadings of 1.1 at% and 1.8 at% (Table S1, Supporting Information) are introduced into NCS, which is denoted as FR‚ÄêNCS. Except for a small amount of aggregates (Figure S2c, Supporting Information), the majority of Fe and Rh are uniformly and atomically dispersed on the NCS shell, highlighted by the blue circles in Figure 2a. It should be noted that ‚Äúhetero‚Äêpair‚Äù atoms are found in Figure 2b. These two atoms in the ‚Äúhetero‚Äêpair‚Äù structure are confirmed to be the Fe and Rh atoms (Figure 2c,d), demonstrating the formation of RhÔ£øFe interbonds. The selected area for the EELS is shown in Figure S6, Supporting Information, excluding the effect from other atoms in a large area.

To further clarify the local configurations around atomically dispersed Rh and Fe, the extended X‚Äêray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) and X‚Äêray absorption near‚Äêedge structure (XANES) measurements are performed. The Fe K‚Äêedge XANES spectra of FR‚ÄêNCS shows clear pre‚Äêedge peaks at ‚âà7112 eV, which is the fingerprint for square‚Äêplanar Fe‚ÄìN4 moieties[17] at the relative lower energy position than 7114 eV in Fe foil (Figure 2e). The Fourier transforms of EXAFS spectra (FT‚ÄêEXAFS, without phase correction) in Figure 2f reveal peaks at R = 1.6 √Ö and R = 2.2 √Ö corresponding to Fe‚ÄìN/C and FeÔ£øFe bonds, respectively. This clearly demonstrates the presence of Fe‚ÄìN4 moieties and the significant reduction in Fe nanoparticles in FR‚ÄêNCS compared to F‚ÄêNCS. The peak at R = 2.5 √Ö of FR‚ÄêNCS shows the shift compared to that of Fe foil. It shows the change of near‚Äêneighbor metal atom shell around the Fe atom, demonstrating the formation of RhÔ£øFe bonding.[36] The peak at R = 1.5 √Ö in the Fourier transforms of EXAFS spectra is due to the contribution from Rh‚ÄìN/C scattering (Figure 2g). Further quantitative EXAFS curve fitting analysis (Table S2, Supporting Information) reveals that the coordination numbers for the Fe edge of the FR‚ÄêNCS sample are 2.0 ¬± 0.5 for Fe‚ÄìFe (reduced from 7.1 ¬± 1.6 in F‚ÄêNCS) at a distance of 2.59 ¬± 0.02 √Ö, and 1.4 ¬± 0.7 for RhÔ£øFe at a distance of 2.59 ¬± 0.04 √Ö (data processing and fitting see Figure S7, Supporting Information). Considering the Fe‚ÄìN4 moieties in the carbon substrate, Rh single atom replaces one C adjacent to N in Fe‚ÄìN4 and binds with neighboring Fe atom (Figure 2h; Figure S8, and Note S2, Supporting Information). The deformation charge density confirms the electron accumulation on the RhÔ£øFe bond as shown in the inset of Figure 2h. This structure is also shown to be thermodynamically stable and is formed with a negative reaction free energy (Note S3 and Figure S9, Supporting Information). Based on the EXAFS fitting results, there still would be other structures besides RhÔ£øFe pair, like metal oligomers, metal single atoms, or bimetallic clusters. As shown in Figure S10, Supporting Information, the metal atoms in RhÔ£øFe pair sites account for over 70% of total atomically dispersed metal atoms, demonstrating the RhÔ£øFe pair as the predominant structure.

Based on the formula of ŒîEatom = (Eatom‚ÄìNCS ‚àí NatomEatom)/Natom, anchor energies (per atom) of dispersed atoms in the NCS structure are calculated to predict the most likely atom‚Äêdispersion structure. The Fe‚Äê, Rh‚Äê, Fe/Rh‚Äê, dimer Fe‚ÄìFe‚Äê, and dimer Rh‚ÄìRh‚Äêanchored NCS (denoted as F‚ÄêNCS, R‚ÄêNCS, FR‚ÄêNCS, Fe/Fe, Rh/Rh for simplicity) are geometrically optimized to calculate anchor energies for comparing their thermodynamic stabilities. As shown in Figure 2i, dual RhÔ£øFe single atoms are preferentially anchored in the NCS structure with the lowest value of the anchor energy, which may be attributed to stronger RhÔ£øFe chemical bonds. Both the experimental and theoretical results indicate that the RhÔ£øFe interbond thermodynamically prefers to form, which facilitates the subsequent dispersion of aggregated metal nanoparticles.

Noble metals (e.g., Pd, Rh, Ir) have been reported to be stabilized by other metal species with higher valence (e.g., La, and Re in oxides, and La in halides) to form hetero‚Äêpairs.[37-39] Here, first‚Äêprinciples calculations based on density functional theory (DFT) combined with experimental results are conducted to reveal the origin of the interactive facilitation effect between Fe and Rh. The experimental studies show that the formation of the RhÔ£øFe chemical bond significantly enhances single atom dispersion with no requirement for high‚Äêtemperature atomization. The chemical bond equivalently provides additional energy for dissociating metal clusters into single atoms. Therefore, our calculations are focused on the local structure around the RhÔ£øFe chemical bond, and the thermodynamic stability contribution due to the RhÔ£øFe chemical bond. Based on previous theoretical calculations, stable Fe10 and Rh7 clusters with closed‚Äêshell electronic structure (magic number) are chosen in our present work. In nature, the cluster size fully depends on the chemical reaction temperature and intrinsic cohesive energy of the bulk. Therefore, although cluster size change may have an impact on chemical reaction thermodynamics, the specific cluster size calculations are still rational to describe the fundamental physical mechanism of atomization.

The local structure around RhÔ£øFe chemical bond is determined by calculating the lowest‚Äêenergy carbon‚Äêvacancy site which may be filled by mobile Rh atoms. At a low‚Äêtemperature condition, the Fe nanoparticle, taking Fe10 as an example, maybe trapped in the N4 defect position by FeÔ£øN4 bond‚Äêforming. The newly formed Fe‚ÄìN4 facilitates the neighboring carbon activation due to bonding electron delocalization from C‚ÄêœÄ to Fe‚Äêd orbitals. As shown in Figure 3a,b, the formation energy associated with four different carbon vacancies (C1, C2, C3, C4) are examined. With the activation caused by Fe‚ÄìN4, the vacancy formation energies (VFE) of these adjacent carbon sites decrease when compared to that of the perfect hexagonal lattice of a carbon plane (7.79 eV). Particularly, the C2 site with the closest distance to the Fe‚ÄìN4 moiety is preferred to be removed from the lattice with a VFE value of 1.69 eV (Figure 3c). These calculations further show that the local structure of C3 RhÔ£øFe N4 is very likely to be formed with four‚Äêligand Rh and five‚Äêligand Fe. The carbon vacancies exist in F‚ÄêNCS, which are also observed in EPR spectrum. The line in the EPR at around 3512 G arises from the unpaired electrons on the carbon atoms of the aromatic rings. Compared with pure NCS, F‚ÄêNCS possesses a weaker ESR signal (Figure 3d), suggesting the decrease of carbon content in F‚ÄêNCS, which corresponds to the formation of carbon vacancy.[40]

The chemical‚Äêbond‚Äêenhanced dissociation mechanism from nanoparticle into a single atom is further investigated. On the one hand, starting from Fe nanoparticles sitting on N4 structures with carbon‚Äêvacancies, the Rh nanoparticles are prohibited to be formed with the Fe‚ÄìN4 (Figure 3e,f). When choosing two control substrates without Fe‚ÄìN4 (Figures S11‚ÄìS14, Supporting Information) to support Rh species (substrate 1: mixed commercial iron and NCS; substrate 2: carbon without nitrogen), the presence of Rh nanoparticles in these two substrates is confirmed (Figure S14, Supporting Information), leading to the conclusion that the pivotal effect of Fe‚ÄìN4 on facilitating the atomic dispersion of Rh. XPS spectrum indicate that metalÔ£øcarbon bond in FR‚ÄêNCS is not observed in F‚ÄêNCS (Figure S15, Supporting Information), confirming the Fe cluster not sitting on carbon and Rh bonded with unsaturated carbon as the metalÔ£øcarbon bonding. The calculated Gibbs free energy has an apparent decrease (Figure 3g), exhibiting the Fe effect in prohibiting the formation of Rh nanoparticle. From XRD patterns, Rh nanoparticle is not formed before and after pyrolysis with the existence of Fe species, while the apparent metallic Rh nanoparticles existed without the assistance of Fe species. Combined with the control samples without Fe‚ÄìN4 structure (Figures S11‚ÄìS14, Supporting Information), the Fe‚ÄìN4 efficiently hinders the formation of Rh even before pyrolysis. On the other hand, with single Rh incorporated into the lattice, the formation of RhÔ£øFeÔ£øN4 bonding weakens the attached Fe10, which induces the shrinkage of Fe10 to Fe9 (Figure 3i,j). During the dispersion process of Fe nanoparticles, the free energy of the whole system reduces by 0.42 eV after Fe atomic dispersion (Figure 3k), showing that the RhÔ£øFe bond facilitates the atomic dispersion of Fe. During the heat‚Äêtreatment, the shift of the peak assigned to metallic Fe indicates the alloying phenomenon, demonstrating the transformation from FeÔ£øFe bond to RhÔ£øFe bond (Figure S16, Supporting Information). In addition, the amount of Fe‚ÄìN4 increases from 7.2% in F‚ÄêNCS to 58.3% when heat‚Äêtreated for 1 h (FR‚ÄêNCS‚Äê1 h) and to 89.3% with 2 h heat‚Äêtreatment (Figure 3l, Figure 1c, and down panel in Figure 1i), supporting the stripping process of Fe cluster. The degradation process of Fe nanoparticles is directly supported by the in situ imaging, showing that the Fe nanoparticles gradually transform and degrade with the increased pyrolysis temperature and prolonged pyrolysis time (Figure S17, Supporting Information).

To evaluate the HER electrocatalytic performance of NCS with the RhÔ£øFe chemical bonds induced atomically dispersed Rh and Fe, rotating disk electrode (RDE) measurements are carried out in comparison to glass carbon electrode (GCE), F‚ÄêNCS, R‚ÄêNCS, Rh/Fe co‚Äêloaded hollow carbon spheres without nitrogen (FR‚ÄêCS), and NCS. Figure 4a shows linear‚Äêsweep voltammograms in the cathodic direction corrected for ohmic potential drop (iR) loss, normalized to the projected geometric area of the electrode. The NCS without metal exhibits a poor HER activity, suggesting the importance of simultaneous presence of both Rh and Fe single atoms. Notably, FR‚ÄêNCS shows outstanding catalytic activity with a positive shift in overpotential at a current density of 10 mA cm‚àí2 (Œ∑10) of 22 mV than that of the commercial Pt/C catalysts. In addition, it is observed that the HER activity of the FR‚ÄêNCS deteriorates with the removal of either atomically dispersed metal species (Fe or Rh). These results demonstrate the synergistic effect that exists between bonded Rh and Fe atoms to significantly boost the activity of NCS. When the activity is normalized to the catalyst mass loading (Figure 4b), the mass activity for the FR‚ÄêNCS catalysts at an overpotential of 0.05 V is 33.7 mA mg‚àí1. The mass activity of FR‚ÄêNCS is 2.2 times of Pt/C, 6.3 times of R‚ÄêNCS, and 45.5 times of F‚ÄêNCS.

As for the reaction paths, the FR‚ÄêNCS sample shows a Tafel slope of 26 mV dec‚àí1, indicating a Volmer‚ÄìTafel mechanism (Figure 4c) with hydrogen desorption as the determining step as illustrated in Note S4, Supporting Information. In contrast, the catalysts without RhÔ£øFe synergy (Fe‚ÄêNCS, R‚ÄêNCS and NCS) exhibit Tafel slopes over 70 mV dec‚àí1 and prefer hydrogen adsorption determined reaction path,[41] reflecting an insufficient bonding strength between the active site and hydrogen. As such, FR‚ÄêNCS possesses neighboring atomic Rh and Fe atoms with RhÔ£øFe bonding, which strongly enhances the hydrogen adsorption process. In addition, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy shows a decreased charge‚Äêtransfer resistance (Rct) for FR‚ÄêNCS (17 Œ©) relative to R‚ÄêNCS (54 Œ©), F‚ÄêNCS (312 Œ©), and FR‚ÄêCS (184 Œ©), in which charge transfer between active sites and proton in electrolyte is facilitated by the existence of neighboring and interacted RhÔ£øFe atoms (Figure 4d). The FR‚ÄêNCS catalyst exhibits the outstanding HER activity and demonstrates the preponderance of bimetallic atomic dispersed Fe and Rh atoms toward a significantly increased activity of immobilized substrate when compared to most of the other single metal atom‚Äêbased HER catalysts,[13, 41-48] and commercial Pt/C (Figure 4e).

We also investigate the stability of the FR‚ÄêNCS catalyst. XPS and M√ñssbauer characterizations are conducted for used and fresh FR‚ÄêNCS (‚Äúused‚Äù refers to the catalyst after one round of reaction). The divergence in M√ñssbauer spectroscopy between the used and fresh FR‚ÄêNCS is negligible when normalized to the amount of Fe‚ÄìN4. This suggests that the atomically dispersed Fe in FR‚ÄêNCS remains nearly unchanged after hydrogen evolution (Figure S18, Supporting Information). Moreover, the XPS Rh 3d spectra of the used FR‚ÄêNCS shows no typical peaks for Rh0, demonstrating the chemical stability of Rh (Figure S19, Supporting Information). After 1000 cycles, only 5 mV negative shift in Œ∑10 is observed compared to the original Œ∑10 value, exhibiting an outstanding long‚Äêterm operational stability (Figure 4f).

To understand the origin of the high catalytic activity of FR‚ÄêNCS, DFT calculations have been performed to reveal a cooperative catalytic mechanism of dual single‚Äêatoms (FR‚ÄêNCS). The possible active sites in the basic catalytic unit FR‚ÄêNCS are illustrated in Figure 4g. Once the hydrated proton is incorporated into water clusters adsorbed in FR‚ÄêNCS (Figure 4h), one hydrogen atom is spontaneously transferred to the Rh atom site, forming a Rh‚ÄìH complex structure (Figure 4h). This indicates that Rh‚Äêmediated hydrogen transfer exhibits an energy drop. As discussed in previous work, the optimal value of ŒîGH* is equal to 0 eV,[49] where adsorbed atomic hydrogen is in a thermoneutral state and can perform efficient proton/electron‚Äêtransfer and hydrogen release. In Figure 4i, atomically dispersed Rh as the active site shows a hydrogen adsorption free energy (ŒîGH*) of ‚àí0.084 eV, much closer to 0 eV compared to Fe and N sites (optimized structures, see Figure S20, Supporting Information), confirming the atomically dispersed Rh contributing as the active site to largely increase the catalytic activity. In fact, the amount of free electronic states near the Fermi level (‚àí1 to 0 eV highlighted by orange rectangular areas) determines the HER catalytic activity in the lower panels in Figure 4j,k. It is found that the Rh‚ÄìFe‚ÄìN moiety has one order of magnitude larger of density states below Fermi level composed predominantly of the Rh 4d orbitals compared to that of neighboring atoms. Therefore, the binding of hydrogen on the Rh site in FR‚ÄêNCS is more favorable than on other atoms. The Rh center is favorable for the adsorption of hydrogen and donates electrons to reduce the proton. The atomically dispersed Fe cannot play the active site in FR‚ÄêNCS configuration, performing a weak interaction with H (ŒîGH of 0.333 eV) and difficulty in H transferring from the electrolyte to atomically dispersed Fe (Figure S21, Supporting Information). The electronic states of the Fe‚Äê3d orbital around the Fermi level (‚àí0.5 to ‚àí1 eV) mainly hybridize with the Rh‚Äê4d and N‚Äê2p orbitals, resulting in less extra free electrons that can transfer to the hydrogen atom for proton reduction. The electronic states of the Rh 4d orbitals between ‚àí1 and 0 eV are dragged to the deep energy level due to hybridization with the 1s orbital of H atoms, leading to electron pairing and hydride formation (lower panels in Figure 4j,k and Figure S22, Supporting Information). Specifically, the absence of Fe atom in R‚ÄêNCS decreases the catalytic activity, exhibiting a ŒîGH of ‚àí0.216 eV and 0.15 e‚àí charge transfer from Rh to H atom. By comparison, the atomically dispersed Fe atom can tune the charge distribution of FR‚ÄêNCS and release extra electrons of Rh atom through RhÔ£øFe bond formation (Figure S23, Supporting Information). The electron redistribution effect of Fe on Rh is also observed in the charge distributions of the upper panels of Figure 4j,k. After H adsorption, the electrons aggregate around atomically dispersed Fe atom (red areas) and Rh shares the electrons with H (dark cyan areas). By theoretical calculations, such excellent activity is attributed mainly to a synergistic effect in FR‚ÄêNCS, whereby the Fe atom facilitates the charge distribution while the active Rh site promotes favorable hydrogen adsorption and evolution.

In summary, a novel mechanism is developed to convert metal nanoparticles to atomically dispersed metal atoms using dual‚Äêmetal interbond (RhÔ£øFe bonding) as a chemical facilitator. The RhÔ£øFe bonding between adjacent Fe and Rh single atoms snips the bonded metal atom from its original nanoparticles via enhanced thermodynamic stability. First‚Äêprinciples calculations and experimental results reveal that the carbon vacancy induced by the neighbored Fe cluster‚ÄêFeN4 structure captures mobile Rh to form a Rh‚ÄìFe interbond. The as‚Äêformed RhÔ£øFe bonding reversely detaches the Fe atom in FeN4 from the Fe cluster‚ÄêFeN4 structure, stabilizing atomically dispersed atoms. The obtained dual single atom Rh‚ÄìFe catalyst exhibits superb electrocatalytic activity for the hydrogen evolution reaction, performing at an ultralow overpotential of 36 mV at 10 mA cm‚àí2 and Tafel slope of 26 mV dec‚àí1, which is superior to 20 wt% Pt/C and other reported single‚Äêatom electrocatalysts. The Rh site is identified as the active site with Fe tuning the electron distribution via the RhÔ£øFe interbond to offer the optimized ŒîGH. This discovery establishes a convenient route to obtain SACs in a wider range, which may even be applied to other metals spontaneously to be atomically dispersed with the assistance from another kind of metal, providing valuable insight for preparing high‚Äêperformance thermally stable SACs.

Experimental Section: Preparation of NCS
The detailed synthetic processes can be found in the previous research.[50] Briefly, 0.74 g 2,4‚Äêdihydroxybenzoic acid and 0.28 g hexamethylentetramine were dissolved in deionized water (DI water) to form solution I. An aqueous solution (80 mL) containing 0.3 g sodium oleate and 0.18 g poly(propylene glycol)‚Äêblock‚Äêpoly(ethylene glycol)‚Äêblock‚Äêpoly(propylene glycol) was added into solution I under slow stirring for 10 min and then transferred to a Teflon‚Äêlined stainless‚Äêsteel autoclave heated at 160 ¬∞C for 8 h. After cooling to room temperature, the products were centrifuged, washed three times with DI water, and dried in vacuum at 50 ¬∞C overnight. The as‚Äêprepared powders were annealed at 650 ¬∞C for 3 h with a ramping rate of 2 ¬∞C min‚àí1 under NH3 atmosphere, followed by further carbonization in N2 at 900 ¬∞C for 2 h.

Preparation of FR‚ÄêNCS, FR‚ÄêNCS‚Äê1 h, FR‚ÄêNCS‚Äê0 h, F‚ÄêNCS, and R‚ÄêNCS
The as‚Äêprepared NCS‚Äê900 (0.1 g) was dissolved in 10 mL ethanol containing 5.8 mg FeCl3. This solution was stirred for 2 h and dried at 80 ¬∞C overnight. The obtained sample was pyrolyzed at 200 ¬∞C for 3 h with a ramping rate of 4 ¬∞C min‚àí1 under 95 vol% Ar/5 vol% H2 atmosphere. The sample was denoted as F‚ÄêNCS. Then, the obtained F‚ÄêNCS (0.1 g) was dispersed in 10 mL DI water containing varying amounts of RhCl3 (0.5, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0 mg), respectively. The obtained solutions were stirred until the liquid was totally evaporated, and underwent the drying process under vacuum overnight, followed by heat treatment at 200 ¬∞C for different hours under 95 vol% Ar/5 vol% H2. The best‚Äêperformance sample with 5.0 mg RhCl3 is denoted as FR‚ÄêNCS (Figure S24, Supporting Information). The samples were heated for 0 h (FR‚ÄêNCS‚Äê0 h) and 1 h (FR‚ÄêNCS‚Äê1 h) as the control samples to verify the atomization process, while the sample with heat treatment for 2 h was denoted as the FR‚ÄêNCS. As for the comparison, R‚ÄêNCS followed the synthetic processes of FR‚ÄêNCS without the step to introduce Fe.

Preparation of FR‚ÄêCS
In order to unveil the effect of N, carbon hollow spheres without N‚Äêdoping were prepared following most of the processes of NCS without pyrolysis in NH3. The left procedures to get FR‚ÄêCS were the same as those of FR‚ÄêNCS.

Structural Characterization
The morphology of the catalysts was observed using a JEOL JEM 2100F transmission electron microscope (TEM) operating at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. This instrument was also equipped with an Oxford Instruments energy‚Äêdispersive X‚Äêray spectrometer (EDS) for compositional analysis. STEM images were acquired by using JEOL 2100F based microscope equipped with delta correctors and cold field‚Äêemission gun. The microscope was operating at 60 kV. The probe current was about 25‚Äì30 pA. The convergence semi‚Äêangle Œ± was 35 mrad and the inner acquisition semi‚Äêangle Œ≤ was 62 mrad. EELS spectrum was taken by using Gatan low‚Äêvoltage image filter Quantum camera. X‚Äêray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were performed on an ESCALAB 250 X‚Äêray photoelectron spectrometer using an Al Ka (hŒΩ = 1486.6 eV) radiation source. 57Fe M√ñŒ≤bauer spectroscopy was performed at room temperature in transmission mode to characterize the various iron species in the catalysts. The spectra were calibrated with respect to Œ±‚ÄêFe‚ÄêStandard. For the identification of iron sites, a Lorentzian site analysis with the program Recoil was made. Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) was recorded on Bruker EMX spectrometer using an Elexsys probe head. Calculation of the spin number was based on a reference powder of Cu(II)TPP with 1 spin per molecule. X‚Äêray absorption fine‚Äêstructure spectroscopy (XAFS) was performed at the Materials Research Collaborative Access Team (MRCAT), Sector 10‚ÄêBM line at the Advanced Photon Source at Argonne National Laboratory.[51] The nanoparticle samples were mixed with boron nitride and PVDF binder and pressed into a 5 mm pellet, then measured in transmission from 200 eV below to 1000 eV above the K absorption edges of Fe (7.11 keV) or Rh (2.3 keV). Metal foils of either Fe or Rh were used to calibrate E0 and served as reference material for the subsequent fitting of the Fourier Transform EXAFS spectra. All data processing and linear combination fitting were performed using the Athena and Artemis programs from the IFEFFIT suite (Note S2 and Figure S7, Supporting Information).[52, 53] The in situ imaging was performed on a Titan Themis Cubed G2 60‚Äê300 microscope using a DENS solutions Climate S3 and Gas flow and Heating System, which comprised a heated sample holder and gas delivery manifold. The dispersed sample was cast on a MEMS in situ heating chip.

Electrochemical Measurements
Electrochemical measurements were carried out in 0.5 m H2SO4 as electrolyte using a typical three‚Äêelectrode cell consisting of a working electrode, a graphite carbon counter electrode and a saturated calomel reference electrode (SCE). The electrochemical cell was connected to an electrochemical workstation (CHI760) coupled with a rotating disk electrode (RDE) system (AFMSRCE3529, Pine Research Instrumentation, USA). A glassy carbon electrode (GCE) covered with catalyst samples was used as the working electrode. The potential, measured against an SCE electrode, was converted to the potential versus the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) according to ERHE = ESCE + EoSCE (0.2412) + 0.059 √ó pH. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was conducted in 0.5 m H2SO4 with a scan rate of 2 mV s‚àí1 under 1500 rpm. The cyclic voltammograms (CVs) were obtained with a scan rate of 100 mV s‚àí1 in the potential range of 0.2‚Äì0.4 V versus RHE. The current density versus potential data plots were corrected for 90% ohmic compensation throughout the system. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were carried out in the same configuration at a potential of 150 mV from 100 KHz to 0.1 Hz. The polarization data for samples in 0.5 m H2SO4 initially and after 1000 CV sweeps between ‚àí0.2 and +0.2 V versus RHE with a scan rate of 50 mV s‚àí1 were used to evaluate the stability performance. The mass activity (mA mg‚àí1) values of samples were calculated from the electrocatalyst loading m (0.5 mg cm‚àí2) and the measured current density j (mA cm‚àí2) at overpotential of 50 mV. The area of electrode was 0.19625 cm‚àí2.

Computational Methods
The spin‚Äêpolarized density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP),[54] with the generalized gradient approximation of Perdew‚ÄìBurke‚ÄìErnzerhof to describe electron exchange and correlation.
